# What A Bitcoin Solo Mining Calculator Actually Tells You

A good Bitcoin solo mining calculator is not there to hype you up.

It is there to slow you down.

Solo mining has one of the most tempting stories in Bitcoin: run your own miner, hit a block, collect the reward.

That story is technically true. It is also incomplete without the odds.

## The Core Inputs

The basic ingredients are simple:

- your hashrate
- current network difficulty
- Bitcoin block reward and fees
- power draw
- electricity cost
- hardware cost

From there, a calculator can estimate things like expected time to find a block, daily chance, monthly chance, yearly chance, expected BTC, power cost, and net expected value.

Those numbers are uncomfortable for small miners. That is exactly why they are useful.

## Expected Time Is Not A Countdown

This is the part people get wrong.

If a calculator says the expected time is extremely long, that does not mean the miner is slowly moving closer to a block.

Every hash is a fresh attempt.

You can get lucky early. You can also run for years and find nothing. The calculator is describing probability, not destiny.

That distinction matters because lottery mining feels different from normal spending. You can talk yourself into it by imagining the jackpot more clearly than the base rate.

The calculator pulls the base rate back into view.

## Why Power Cost Still Matters

For tiny miners, power cost may be small enough that the hobby feels harmless.

For larger hobby setups, power can become the quiet leak.

Even when the odds are the main story, electricity still affects whether a setup is a fun experiment or a bad habit. A calculator that includes power cost forces the question:

"Would I still run this if I never hit a block?"

That is probably the right question.
